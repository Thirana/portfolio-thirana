# Product

## Register

brand

## Users

Three overlapping audiences, each visiting to make a decision:

- **Engineering hiring managers and EMs**: deciding whether to advance to an interview. They read fast, pattern-match on signals of craft and operational depth, and distrust self-promotion.
- **Technical recruiters**: doing a first pass to qualify and pass along. Looking for clear evidence of backend scope and delivery.
- **Peers and collaborators**: other engineers curious about a project, a blog post, or a potential working relationship. They have the most patience and care most about the writing and technical depth.

All three reward restraint. None of them need to be sold to.

## Product Purpose

Personal portfolio for Thirana Embuldeniya, a backend engineer focused on reliability, data systems, and architecture. The site exists to signal engineering credibility and taste to people who might want to hire or collaborate. Success means someone reaches out, or a conversation advances that would not have happened otherwise.

## Brand Personality

Credible, Direct, Precise.

The tone is that of an engineer who has shipped real things and doesn't need to prove it with decoration. Quiet confidence. The writing is the product; the UI scaffolds it.

## Anti-references

- Generic dev portfolio templates: rainbow skill bars, animated skill percentage counters, typewriter "I am a [role]" hero effects, identical icon-grid project cards.
- Startup landing page energy: hero metrics, gradient CTAs, "helping teams ship faster" copy, social proof banners.
- Over-designed portfolios that signal designer, not engineer.

## Design Principles

1. **Work is the hero.** Projects and writing are the real content. The UI's job is to get out of the way and let them land.
2. **Signal without noise.** Every visual element must earn its place. Credibility comes from restraint, not decoration.
3. **Speak to engineers.** The site assumes a technically literate reader. It does not over-explain or dumb down.
4. **Show, don't describe.** Surface concrete evidence (projects, outcomes, writing) rather than adjectives about work.
5. **Practice what you preach.** A backend engineer's site should be structurally clean, fast, and well-considered. Technical craft is visible in the implementation, not just the copy.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

WCAG AA baseline. Keyboard navigable. No motion for users with reduced-motion preferences. Sufficient contrast on all text.
